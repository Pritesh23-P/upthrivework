## The Difference Between a Good and a Great ML Project

In our experience working with hundreds of students on AI/ML projects, the difference between a good grade and a great grade almost always comes down to the same handful of mistakes. These aren't complex problems — they're fundamentals that get overlooked under deadline pressure.

Here are the five most common mistakes we see, and exactly how to fix them.

## Mistake 1: Skipping Exploratory Data Analysis (EDA)

Too many students jump straight from loading a dataset to building a model. This is one of the most costly shortcuts you can take.

EDA is not optional — it is the foundation of any serious ML project. Before you train a single model, you need to understand your data deeply: its distribution, missing values, outliers, class imbalances, and feature correlations.

**The fix:** Dedicate at least 20-30% of your project time to EDA. Use Pandas, Matplotlib, and Seaborn to visualize your data thoroughly. Document your findings — assessors want to see that you understand your data before you model it.

## Mistake 2: Not Handling Class Imbalance

If your dataset has significantly more samples of one class than another — which is extremely common in real-world datasets — training a model without addressing this will produce misleading results. A model that predicts the majority class every time can achieve 90% accuracy while being completely useless.

**The fix:** Use techniques like SMOTE for oversampling, class weighting in your model parameters, or undersampling the majority class. Always evaluate imbalanced datasets using precision, recall, F1-score, and AUC-ROC — not just accuracy.

## Mistake 3: Data Leakage

Data leakage is when information from outside your training set is used to build or evaluate your model. It produces artificially inflated performance metrics that collapse the moment the model hits real data. This is one of the most serious mistakes in any ML project.

**The fix:** Always split your data into train and test sets before any preprocessing. Apply scaling, encoding, and feature selection only on the training set, then transform the test set using the parameters learned from training. Use Scikit-learn Pipelines to enforce this correctly.

## Mistake 4: No Model Explainability

Building a black-box model and reporting its accuracy is no longer sufficient for academic ML projects. Assessors increasingly expect students to demonstrate that they understand why their model makes the predictions it does.

**The fix:** Use SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations) or LIME to provide feature importance and prediction explanations. Even a basic feature importance plot from your tree-based model goes a long way in demonstrating depth of understanding.

## Mistake 5: Poor Project Structure and Documentation

Even a technically excellent ML project will lose marks if the code is messy, undocumented, and impossible to follow. Your assessor needs to be able to run your code, understand your methodology, and reproduce your results.

**The fix:** Structure your project clearly with separate notebooks or scripts for EDA, preprocessing, modelling, and evaluation. Write clear comments and docstrings. Include a well-written README with setup instructions. Use a requirements.txt to document dependencies. Make your results fully reproducible by setting random seeds.

## Summary

Avoiding these five mistakes won't just improve your grade — it will make you a significantly better data scientist. These are the same standards applied in industry ML projects, and developing these habits now will serve you throughout your career.
