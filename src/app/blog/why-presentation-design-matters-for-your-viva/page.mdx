## Your Presentation Is Your First Impression

In a viva or academic presentation, your slides are not just visual aids — they are a direct reflection of how seriously you take your work. A poorly designed presentation signals carelessness. A well-designed one signals professionalism, clarity of thought, and confidence.

After designing hundreds of academic presentations for students across undergraduate, postgraduate, and PhD levels, here's what we've learned about what separates presentations that impress from those that underwhelm.

## 1. Structure Is Everything

The most common mistake in academic presentations is having no clear narrative flow. Your audience — whether it's one supervisor or a full dissertation committee — needs to be guided through your work in a logical, coherent sequence.

A strong academic presentation follows this structure:

- **Opening:** What is the problem and why does it matter?
- **Context:** What does the existing literature say?
- **Methodology:** What did you do and why?
- **Results:** What did you find?
- **Discussion:** What does it mean?
- **Conclusion:** What are the key takeaways and future directions?

Every slide should serve one of these sections. If a slide doesn't clearly fit, cut it.

## 2. One Idea Per Slide

This is the single most impactful change most students can make. Cramming multiple ideas, bullet points, and charts onto a single slide forces your audience to choose between reading your slides and listening to you. They cannot do both.

Commit to one clear idea per slide. Use the headline of the slide to state the key point, and use the visual or body of the slide to support it. Your slides should reinforce what you're saying — not replace it.

## 3. Data Visualizations Must Be Clear and Honest

For technical and scientific presentations, your charts and graphs carry a significant portion of your argument. A poorly labelled, cluttered, or misleading visualization can undermine an otherwise strong result.

Every visualization should have a clear title that states what it shows, labelled axes with units, a legend where necessary, and enough whitespace to be readable from the back of the room. Avoid 3D charts — they distort perception and add no informational value.

## 4. Design Consistency Builds Credibility

Using five different font sizes, three different colour schemes, and inconsistent slide layouts across your presentation gives the impression of a rushed, unpolished piece of work — regardless of how strong your research is.

Pick a clean, professional colour palette (two to three colours maximum), use one or two font families consistently throughout, and maintain consistent margins, spacing, and alignment on every slide. Consistency signals attention to detail — a quality every academic assessor values.

## 5. Less Text, More Confidence

If your slides are full of text, you will be tempted to read from them. Reading from your slides is one of the fastest ways to lose the confidence of your audience. It signals that you don't know your material well enough to speak to it naturally.

Replace text-heavy slides with visuals, diagrams, and key data points. Let your spoken words carry the detail. Your slides should be a visual anchor for your audience — not a script for you.

## Final Thoughts

A great viva presentation is the result of clear thinking, deliberate structure, and careful design. It is absolutely a learnable skill, and the investment in getting it right is always worth it.
